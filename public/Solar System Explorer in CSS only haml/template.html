Star

Sun

The heart of our solar system

The Sun is the star at the center of the Solar System — a nearly perfect sphere of hot plasma, with internal convective motion that generates a magnetic field via a dynamo process.

The Sun accounts for 99.86% of the total mass in the solar system — roughly 330,000 times the mass of Earth.

Planet

Mercury

The swift-footed messenger

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun. It circles the Sun faster than all other planets, which is why Romans named it after their swift-footed messenger god.

The Sumerians knew of Mercury since at least 5,000 years ago. Heraclitus, around 500 B.C., correctly thought that both Mercury and Venus orbited the Sun, not Earth.

A year on Mercury is just 88 Earth days, yet a solar day (noon to noon) lasts 176 Earth days — longer than its own year.

Smallest planet in the Solar System

Mercury is just 4,879 km across its equator, compared to 12,742 km for Earth. Despite its small size, it is the second densest planet — largely due to its heavy metallic core.

Mercury has wrinkles

As its iron core cooled and contracted, the surface buckled into ridges called Lobate Scarps — up to a mile high and hundreds of miles long.

Planet

Venus

Brightest object in the night sky

Venus, the second planet from the Sun, is named for the Roman goddess of love and beauty. It may have been named for the most beautiful deity because it shone the brightest of the five planets known to ancient astronomers.

In ancient times, Venus was thought to be two different stars — the evening star and the morning star. Further space-age observations reveal a hellish, crushing environment where spacecraft don't survive long on the surface.

A day on Venus lasts longer than its year. It takes 243 Earth days to rotate once, but only 225 days to orbit the Sun.

Spins backwards

Venus rotates in the opposite direction to most planets — a retrograde rotation — possibly caused by an ancient collision with a large asteroid.

Crushing atmosphere

Atmospheric pressure on Venus is 92 times greater than Earth's, equivalent to the pressure experienced 900 metres underwater.

Planet

Earth

Our pale blue dot

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known world to harbour life. It is the only planet with free oxygen in its atmosphere, liquid oceans on its surface, and a rich biosphere.

Earth is the fifth largest planet — smaller than the four gas giants, but larger than the other rocky planets.

Earth's rotation is gradually slowing by about 17 milliseconds per century. In 140 million years, a day will be 25 hours long.

Powerful magnetic field

Earth's nickel-iron core generates a magnetic field that deflects harmful solar wind and cosmic radiation, making complex life possible on the surface.

The Moon

Relative to the size of its parent planet, the Moon is the largest natural satellite in the solar system. Its gravitational pull stabilises Earth's axial tilt, helping maintain a stable climate over geological timescales.

Planet

Mars

The Red Planet

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun. The Romans named it after their god of war. The Egyptians called it "Her Desher" — the red one — while ancient Chinese astronomers dubbed it "the fire star."

Olympus Mons is the tallest mountain in the solar system — 21 km high and 600 km in diameter, nearly three times the height of Everest.

Similar landmass to Earth

Despite only 15% of Earth's volume and 10% of its mass, Mars has roughly the same landmass — because two thirds of Earth's surface is covered in water. Martian gravity is 37% of Earth's.

Planetary dust storms

Mars experiences the largest dust storms in the solar system, capable of blanketing the entire planet for months at a time.

Planet

Jupiter

King of the planets

Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system. Named after the king of the Roman gods, it was also called Zeus by the ancient Greeks. In 1610, Galileo's discovery of four of its moons revolutionised our understanding of the universe.

Galileo's observation that celestial bodies orbit something other than Earth was crucial support for the Copernican heliocentric model.

Jupiter has the shortest day of all planets — rotating once every 9 hours and 55 minutes, despite being 11 times Earth's diameter.

Fourth brightest object in the sky

Only the Sun, Moon, and Venus are brighter. Jupiter is one of five planets visible to the naked eye from Earth.

95 known moons

Jupiter's Galilean moons — Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto — are four of the most geologically fascinating worlds in the solar system, with Io being the most volcanically active body we know of.

Planet

Saturn

Lord of the rings

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest. Saturn was the Roman name for Cronus, lord of the Titans. Saturn is even the root of the English word "Saturday."

The most distant planet visible to the naked eye, Saturn is most remarkable through a telescope — its ring system has no equal in the solar system.

Saturn is the flattest planet — its polar diameter is only 90% of its equatorial diameter due to its low density and rapid rotation.

Less dense than water

Saturn has the lowest density of any planet — about 0.69 g/cm³, compared to water at 1 g/cm³. If you could find an ocean large enough, Saturn would float.

146 moons

Saturn has more moons than any other planet. Titan, its largest, is the only moon in the solar system with a thick atmosphere and liquid lakes — of methane — on its surface.

Planet

Uranus

The tilted ice giant

Uranus is the seventh planet and the first to be discovered by scientists. Although visible to the naked eye, it was long mistaken for a dim star because of its slow orbit.

British astronomer William Herschel discovered Uranus accidentally on March 13, 1781, while surveying the sky with his telescope. He first thought it was a comet.

Uranus has a dramatic 98° axial tilt — it effectively rolls around the Sun on its side. During some orbital positions, one pole faces the Sun directly for 42 years.

An ice giant

Unlike the gas giants Jupiter and Saturn, Uranus is mostly composed of icy materials — water, methane, and ammonia — above a rocky core. Methane in the atmosphere gives it its distinctive pale blue-green colour.

Retrograde rotation

Uranus rotates in the opposite direction to the Sun — one of only two planets to do so — completing one rotation every 17 hours 14 minutes.

Planet

Neptune

The windswept ice giant

Neptune is the eighth planet from the Sun and the first to be predicted mathematically before it was ever observed. Irregularities in Uranus's orbit led astronomers to deduce Neptune's existence, and it was finally spotted on September 23, 1846.

Only one mission has flown by Neptune — Voyager 2 in 1989 — leaving many mysteries about this distant, azure world.

Neptune has the fastest winds in the solar system — reaching up to 2,100 km/h, more than ten times the speed of Earth's most powerful hurricanes.

Smallest ice giant

Despite being smaller than Uranus, Neptune has greater mass. Below its hydrogen-helium atmosphere lies a mantle of water, ammonia, and methane ices above a rocky core.

Blue from methane

Methane in Neptune's upper atmosphere absorbs red light, reflecting blue — giving the planet its vivid, deep azure colour. Thin, high clouds drift above the blue haze.

Dwarf Planet

Pluto

King of the Kuiper Belt

Pluto, once the ninth planet, is now the largest known dwarf planet in the solar system. It is also one of the largest known members of the Kuiper Belt — a vast region of icy bodies beyond Neptune.

In 2006, the IAU reclassified Pluto as a dwarf planet — a change that sparked debate in the scientific community and public alike. In 2017, a research group proposed expanding the planet count to roughly 100 based on a new "round objects in space" definition.

Pluto was discovered on February 18, 1930 by the Lowell Observatory. In the 76 years before its reclassification, it completed less than a third of one orbit around the Sun.

Named by an 11-year-old

Venetia Burney, an eleven-year-old schoolgirl from Oxford, England, proposed the name Pluto — after the Greek god of the underworld — upon learning of the discovery.

Five moons

Pluto has five known moons: Charon (1978), Nix and Hydra (2005), Kerberos (2011), and Styx (2012). Charon is so large relative to Pluto that the two are sometimes considered a double dwarf planet system.
