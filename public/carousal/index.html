SOLAR SYSTEM
SUN
The Sun is a massive star at the center of our Solar System, containing 99.86% of its total mass. Its core reaches 15 million°C, powering life on Earth through nuclear fusion.
MERCURY
Mercury is the smallest planet and closest to the Sun. It has no atmosphere, causing extreme temperature swings — scorching hot by day and freezing cold at night.
VENUS
Venus is the hottest planet, with surface temperatures reaching 465°C due to its thick CO₂ atmosphere. It rotates backwards, making the Sun rise in the west.
EARTH
Earth is the only known planet to harbor life. Liquid water, a protective atmosphere, and a magnetic field make it uniquely suited for complex organisms.
MOON
Earth's only natural satellite, the Moon stabilizes our planet's axial tilt and drives ocean tides. It is the only celestial body beyond Earth visited by humans.
MARS
Known as the Red Planet due to iron oxide on its surface, Mars has the tallest volcano in the Solar System — Olympus Mons — and is actively explored for signs of past life.
JUPITER
Jupiter is the largest planet, so massive it could fit all others inside it. Its Great Red Spot is a storm that has raged for over 350 years.
SATURN
Saturn is famous for its stunning rings made of ice and rock. Despite being the second largest planet, it is so low in density that it would float on water.
URANUS
Uranus rotates on its side with a 98° axial tilt, making its seasons extreme. It appears blue-green due to methane and is the coldest planet in the Solar System.
NEPTUNE
Neptune is the farthest and windiest planet, with speeds reaching 2,100 km/h. Its largest moon Triton uniquely orbits in the opposite direction of the planet's rotation.
